The beech blight aphid forms large, densely packed colonies on the bark of Taxodium and beech trunks, preferring the lower trunk and major branches rather than the leafy canopy tips. Their conspicuous white woolly wax and clustering behaviour make them highly visible on bark surfaces. In Amsterdam, Taxodium distichum appears in park and canal-edge plantings where this aphid can establish. Because colonies sit on the bark surface rather than within foliage, tree-bark is more accurate than tree-canopy, distinguishing them from leaf-feeding species. They are sessile and idle-only.
