The Marbled Orchard Tortrix is a small tortricid moth whose larvae are leaf-rollers and bud-borers on apple, pear, hawthorn, and other rosaceous trees, making tree-canopy the clear dominant habitat. Adults rest by day on bark and leaves with characteristic tortrix wing posture (roof-like), relying on their marbled grey-and-white pattern for camouflage against lichen-covered bark. The default insect placement of flower-visitor is incorrect for a species entirely dependent on tree foliage and buds. Adults fly in June and July; larvae overwinter as first instars in bark crevices. Amsterdam observations come from orchard gardens, hawthorn-lined avenues, and parks with mature rosaceous trees.
