Bagworm moths spend most of their larval life encased in a silk-and-debris bag anchored to the foliage or bark of conifers such as Thuja in Amsterdam gardens and parks. The bag-carrying larva moves very slowly along branches and twigs, effectively appearing stationary at map scale, while the wingless adult female never leaves her case at all. Tree-canopy is the dominant placement because bags are most visible hanging from leafy branchlets; tree-bark is secondary for larvae descending to pupate. The insect default of flower-visitor / flower-bobber does not apply to a sessile bark-and-foliage feeder, so idle-only is the correct movement override.
