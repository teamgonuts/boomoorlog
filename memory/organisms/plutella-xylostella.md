The Diamond-back Moth is one of the world's most abundant moths, a tiny (15 mm wingspan) species whose larvae are serious pests of brassica crops globally. In Amsterdam it occurs wherever cabbages, mustards, and related Brassicaceae grow, from allotment gardens to ruderal wasteland patches. Adults rest with wings folded to show the characteristic diamond pattern along the back, and are most active at dusk. It is not a flower visitor; the default insect placement of flower-visitor is incorrect. The moth is continuously brooded in warm years, with multiple generations from spring through autumn. Its exceptionally rapid evolution of insecticide resistance has made it a model organism in pest management research.
