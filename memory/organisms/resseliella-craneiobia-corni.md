The dogwood gall midge lays its eggs in the leaf margins and shoot tips of Cornus, inducing distinctive pod-like leaf-roll galls in the canopy of ornamental Cornus trees used in Amsterdam street plantings and parks. The larvae develop entirely within the gall tissue and are immobile; the adult midges are tiny and short-lived. Tree-canopy is the correct placement because galls form on foliage, and idle-only captures the effectively stationary appearance of gall-bound larvae. The insect default of flower-visitor / flower-bobber does not apply to a gall-inducing leaf miner, requiring the override.
