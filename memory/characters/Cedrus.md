# Cedrus — Cedar (Cedrus)

**Archetype (draft):** Bruiser
**Amsterdam trees:** 205

## Personality
An ancient mountain warlord who stood on Himalayan ridges long before Amsterdam existed — resin-armoured, slow to grow, impossible to rot, and absolutely certain he has seen it all before.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 30–40 m typical; up to 65 m recorded (Cedrus deodara up to 50 m)
- **Lifespan:** 800–1,000+ years; documented ring-counted specimens to 619 yr; widely reported over 1,000 yr
- **Wood:** ~560 kg/m³ air-dry (Cedrus deodara); dense, resinous, naturally durable softwood
- **Growth speed:** Medium (fast early — rapid until ~45–50 yr, then extremely slow)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Native to western Himalayas and Mediterranean mountains (1,300–3,000 m altitude); strongly resinous wood resists decay and insects; flat-topped layered crown develops only with great age; culturally sacred in Lebanon (national symbol); narrow wild range — four species only; threatened in native habitat by climate change and overgrazing

## Combat flavor
Cedrus hits slow but each blow carries the weight of centuries — dense resinous wood and extreme durability translate to a high-armour, high-damage bruiser who barely notices small foes. Its age-gated power (slow early growth, then near invincibility) could manifest as a passive that scales up the longer Cedrus survives a wave.

## Living creatures
Cedrus is a non-native conifer in NW Europe with markedly lower invertebrate value than native broadleaves — its insect-repellent aromatic resin keeps the fauna small but predictable, dominated by a couple of host-specific aphids and cavity/nest users.

- **Cedar aphid (Cinara cedri)** — thousands to tens of thousands per heavily infested mature tree at peak; produces copious honeydew and sooty mould. [First record Cinara cedri Bulgaria (ResearchGate)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/355204224_First_record_of_Cinara_Cinara_cedri_cedri_Mimeur_Hemiptera_Aphididae_in_Bulgaria)
- **Cedar woolly aphid (Cedrobium laportei)** — hundreds to low thousands on susceptible C. deodara / C. atlantica individuals; two main aphid pests on the genus. [INRAE Cedrobium/Cinara susceptibility study](https://hal.inrae.fr/hal-02728526)
- **Aphid parasitoid wasps (Pauesia anatolica and relatives)** — dozens per tree during aphid outbreaks; specialist parasitoids of Cinara cedri. [Pauesia anatolica description (Springer)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF02981399)
- **Ectomycorrhizal fungi (Thelephoraceae, Trichophaea spp.)** — ~13–19 species in the root zone of a mature tree; not animals but the dominant living partners. [ECM community in Cedrus deodara (PMC)](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10502312/)
- **Goldcrest (Regulus regulus)** — 1–2 pairs per mature cedar; conifer-specialist that builds hammock nests in dense needled branches. [RSPB: Goldcrest](https://www.rspb.org.uk/birds-and-wildlife/goldcrest)
- **Coal Tit (Periparus ater)** — 1 pair per mature cedar where cavities exist; conifer-loving tit, gleans aphids and small invertebrates. [Plantura: Coal tit](https://plantura.garden/uk/garden-birds/guides/coal-tit)
- **Treecreeper (Certhia familiaris)** — 1 individual regularly working a mature trunk; probes thick fissured cedar bark for spiders and insects. [Birdfact: Goldcrest vs Firecrest (mixed-flock context)](https://www.birdfact.com/articles/goldcrest-or-firecrest)
- **Woodpeckers (Dendrocopos major)** — 0–1 pairs nesting in older specimens with heart-rot; "birds, including the woodpecker, use it for nesting." [NC Extension: Cedrus deodara](https://plants.ces.ncsu.edu/plants/cedrus-deodara/)
- **Squirrels (Sciurus spp.)** — 1–2 working a cone-bearing mature tree; "squirrels and birds are especially attracted to the cones." [NC Extension: Cedrus deodara](https://plants.ces.ncsu.edu/plants/cedrus-deodara/)
- **Roosting bats (Pipistrellus, Nyctalus)** — 0–1 small colonies in old cedars with bark splits or cavities; bats use any old tree with the right structure. [Bat Conservation Trust: roosts in trees](https://www.bats.org.uk/about-bats/where-do-bats-live/bat-roosts/roosts-in-trees)
