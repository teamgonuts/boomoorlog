# Quercus — Oak (Eik)

**Archetype (draft):** Bruiser
**Amsterdam trees:** 18085

## Personality
Ancient, unbending, and host to an empire of 400 insect species — the Oak doesn't just fight, it brings an entire ecosystem to war.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 20–40 m; exceptional specimens reach 45 m
- **Lifespan:** Typically 500–800 years; exceptional individuals exceed 1000 years; one of the longest-lived temperate broadleaves
- **Wood:** ~720 kg/m³ (air-dry, Quercus robur); Janka ~1290 lbf (red oak) to 1350 lbf (white oak); very hard, ring-porous, naturally durable due to high tannin content; tannins also waterproof and resist rot
- **Growth speed:** Slow (especially in maturity)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Keystone species supporting 400+ insect species in Europe; acorns are a critical mast crop for mammals and birds; tannins in bark and wood are antimicrobial and historically used for leather tanning; very slow heartwood development but extremely dense and durable once formed; English oak (Q. robur) is a cultural symbol of endurance and national identity across northern Europe; some ancient oaks are heritage monuments

## Combat flavor
The slowest-growing Bruiser but the hardest-hitting — millennial lifespan and tannin-rich wood justify exceptional armor and damage stats, while its keystone ecology lends itself to a passive "rally allies" aura ability.

## Living creatures
Quercus hosts more invertebrate species than any other tree in northern Europe — Kennedy & Southwood (1984) list ~423 phytophagous insects on British oak, plus ~1000 oak-gall wasp species globally and dozens of vertebrate associates.

- **Springtails & oribatid mites (Collembola / Oribatida)** — tens of thousands per mature tree across bark crevices, leaf litter and canopy moss; mites alone hit ~200,000/m² in forest soils beneath the tree. [forest arthropod density](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.abq4049)
- **Winter moth caterpillars (Operophtera brumata)** — up to ~150,000 defoliating larvae on a heavily infested mature oak in May. [UMass winter moth](https://www.umass.edu/agriculture-food-environment/fruit/news/winter-moth-operophtera-brumata)
- **Oak aphids (Tuberculatus / Lachnus / Thelaxes spp.)** — many thousands to tens of thousands per canopy; produce the "sticky oak" honeydew rain in summer. [BYGL sticky oaks](https://bygl.osu.edu/node/1784)
- **Oak gall wasps (Cynipidae, ~30+ spp. per tree)** — hundreds to low thousands of galls per mature tree (knopper, marble, spangle, cherry, oak-apple); the tribe Cynipini holds ~1000 species worldwide, all oak-specialists. [oak gall wasps overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cynipidae)
- **Acorn weevils (Curculio glandium / C. elephas)** — in a mast year, a single mature oak's ~10,000+ acorns can carry weevil larvae in 30–60% of them. [Wildlife Trusts acorn weevil](https://www.wildlifetrusts.org/wildlife-explorer/invertebrates/beetles/acorn-weevil)
- **Purple hairstreak butterfly (Favonius quercus)** — UK's only obligate-oak butterfly; a mature oak typically hosts a resident colony of ~20–200 adults in midsummer. [Butterfly Conservation](https://butterfly-conservation.org/butterflies/purple-hairstreak)
- **Blue & Great Tits (Cyanistes caeruleus / Parus major)** — usually 1–3 nesting pairs per mature oak, timed to the winter-moth caterpillar peak; a brood eats ~10,000 caterpillars in 3 weeks. [Woodland Trust oak wildlife](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/oak-tree-wildlife/)
- **Eurasian Jay (Garrulus glandarius)** — 1–few resident birds per veteran oak in autumn; a single jay caches ~4,500 acorns per season. [Woodland Trust oak wildlife](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/oak-tree-wildlife/)
- **Roosting bats (Pipistrellus / Myotis / Nyctalus)** — veteran oaks with woodpecker holes and loose bark commonly host 1 to a few roosting bats; large hollow trees can shelter maternity colonies of 20–50. [Woodland Trust oak wildlife](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/oak-tree-wildlife/)
- **Grey & Red Squirrels (Sciurus carolinensis / S. vulgaris)** — 1–2 individuals routinely forage a mature oak in mast years; a single squirrel buries ~3,000–10,000 acorns per autumn. [PLOS oak canopy predation](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0017857)
