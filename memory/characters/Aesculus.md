# Aesculus — Horse chestnut (Paardenkastanje)

**Archetype (draft):** Juggernaut
**Amsterdam trees:** 3805

## Personality
A grand, round-crowned bruiser beloved by schoolchildren and feared by horses — toxic from root to conker, spectacularly large, and absolutely not interested in being useful timber.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 20–35 m typical; up to 39 m max
- **Lifespan:** Up to 300 years
- **Wood:** ~500 kg/m³ air-dry; Janka hardness ~3,630 N (820 lbf) — soft and light for a broadleaf, low commercial value
- **Growth speed:** Medium
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Native to Balkan mountain forests (Greece, Albania, 228–1,485 m altitude); all parts contain toxic saponin glycoside aesculin (seeds, bark, leaves — lethal to dogs and horses if eaten); large domed crown with massive canopy footprint; heavy conker seeds gravity-dispersed; important nectar source for bees; no significant invasive profile outside cultivation; culturally iconic in northern Europe (conker games, park trees)

## Combat flavor
Aesculus is a slow, immovable juggernaut — soft wood means it doesn't hit the hardest, but its sheer bulk and canopy dominance give it high health and area-suppression. Its toxicity is the signature ability: a poison-on-contact aura that damages any unit that lingers too close, representing the aesculin in fallen conkers and leaf litter.

## Living creatures
Aesculus has an unusually thin native invertebrate community in NW Europe — it's not from here — but is now dominated by one runaway invasive miner, plus a strong pollinator and bird community drawn to its huge flower panicles and conker crop.

- **Horse-chestnut leaf miner (Cameraria ohridella / Gracillariidae)** — easily 100,000+ mines per mature tree by late summer; the defining herbivore of every Dutch horse-chestnut. [Forest Research: Cameraria ohridella](https://www.forestresearch.gov.uk/tools-and-resources/fthr/pest-and-disease-resources/horse-chestnut-leaf-miner-cameraria-ohridella/)
- **Honey bees (Apis mellifera)** — hundreds of foragers at peak bloom; horse-chestnut nectar can be a significant May honey crop. [Beespoke: Bee Trees – Horse Chestnut](http://beespoke.info/2014/02/10/bee-trees-horse-chestnut/)
- **Bumblebees (Bombus terrestris / B. hortorum / Apidae)** — dozens working a panicle simultaneously; they're the key cross-pollinator, working spikes bottom-to-top. [Biological Flora: Aesculus hippocastanum](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1365-2745.13116)
- **Aesculus scale (Pulvinaria regalis / Coccidae)** — often thousands of conspicuous white egg-masses per trunk on city horse-chestnuts; a defining urban pest. [RHS: Horse chestnut scale](https://www.rhs.org.uk/biodiversity/horse-chestnut-scale)
- **Wood-boring beetles & longhorns (Cerambycidae, in declining trees)** — a few dozen larvae per stressed tree, especially where bleeding canker has weakened the trunk. [Biological Flora: Aesculus hippocastanum](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1365-2745.13116)
- **Hoverflies & solitary bees (Syrphidae, Andrena)** — dozens at the flowers; horse-chestnut's nectar guides actually change colour from yellow to red once flowers have been visited. [Biological Flora: Aesculus hippocastanum](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1365-2745.13116)
- **Blue & Great Tits (Cyanistes / Parus)** — 1 nesting pair per mature tree; they hammer Cameraria mines as a food source for chicks, partially compensating for the leaf damage. [Woodland Trust: Horse chestnut](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/horse-chestnut/)
- **Eurasian Jay (Garrulus glandarius)** — 1–2 birds caching conkers in autumn; jays plus squirrels move and bury most of the seed crop. [Woodland Trust: Horse chestnut](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/horse-chestnut/)
- **Eurasian Grey Squirrel (Sciurus carolinensis)** — 1–3 individuals; main mammalian harvester of conkers, despite the saponin toxicity that deters horses and dogs. [Woodland Trust: Horse chestnut](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/horse-chestnut/)
- **Roosting bats (Pipistrellus, Nyctalus / Vespertilionidae)** — 1 roosting bat or small colony in older trees with cavities; mature horse-chestnuts in Amsterdam parks often hollow out and host pipistrelles. [Bat Conservation Trust: Bats and woodland](https://www.bats.org.uk/our-work/landscapes-for-bats/bats-and-woodland)
