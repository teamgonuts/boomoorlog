# Pterocarya — Wingnut (Vleugelnoot)

**Archetype (draft):** Juggernaut (Legendary)
**Amsterdam trees:** 1815

## Personality
Built like a freight train from the walnut family, the Wingnut sends winged seeds raining down like shrapnel while its massive canopy and deep riverside roots make it nearly impossible to uproot.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 20–30 m typical; Caucasian wingnut (P. fraxinifolia) can exceed 30 m (up to ~35 m); fastest-recorded growth of 6 ft (~1.8 m) per year in youth
- **Lifespan:** 200–260+ years for P. fraxinifolia
- **Wood:** Basic specific gravity ~0.35 (equates to ~450–500 kg/m³ air-dry estimate); Brinell hardness >5 kg/mm²; comparable to light commercial hardwoods; used for plywood, matches, furniture. Density label: estimated air-dry ~460 kg/m³
- **Growth speed:** Fast (especially in youth; among fastest deciduous hardwoods)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Member of Juglandaceae (walnut family) — roots produce juglone-like allelopathic compounds suppressing nearby plants; strongly riparian, stabilises riverbanks; large pinnate leaves (up to 90 cm); prolific winged-nutlet seed dispersal; spreads aggressively by root suckers; native to Asia/Caucasus region; uncommon outside specialist collections in the West

## Combat flavor
The Juggernaut label fits: fast growth to massive size, allelopathic root zone acts as area-denial, and a seed-rain special attack would suit its prolific winged fruit. Moderate wood density means it can swing with real authority without being sluggish.

## Living creatures
A non-native Asian member of the walnut family (Juglandaceae) with juglone-like allelopathy — Pterocarya carries no specialist NW European herbivores; its biological value in Amsterdam is mostly as massive structural habitat: huge canopy, riparian shade, deep cavities in old trunks, and a heavy autumn rain of winged nutlets.

- **Aphids (Aphidoidea, incl. Myzus persicae)** — thousands per tree on tender shoots, producing leaf curl, honeydew and sooty mould. [Botanical Realm Pterocarya](https://www.botanicalrealm.com/plant-identification/caucasian-wingnut-pterocarya-fraxinifolia/)
- **Spider mites (Tetranychidae)** — tens of thousands per tree in hot dry city summers; common Juglandaceae pest. [Botanical Realm Pterocarya](https://www.botanicalrealm.com/plant-identification/caucasian-wingnut-pterocarya-fraxinifolia/)
- **Generalist caterpillars (winter moth, tortrix spp.)** — hundreds per tree in May; juglone-like compounds keep specialist Lepidoptera away. [NTS plant of the month](https://www.nts.org.uk/stories/plant-of-the-month-pterocarya-fraxinifolia)
- **Honeybees & bumblebees (Apis, Bombus)** — opportunistic visits to early-spring catkins; modest pollen value, dozens per tree. [Trees and Shrubs Online Pterocarya](https://www.treesandshrubsonline.org/articles/pterocarya/)
- **Bark-foraging birds (Treecreeper, Nuthatch — Certhia / Sitta)** — 1–2 birds working the deeply furrowed bark for insects; the textured trunk is ideal for them. [Portland.gov Pterocarya](https://www.portland.gov/trees/pterocarya-fraxinifolia)
- **Wood pigeon / Magpie / Carrion crow** — corvids and pigeons routinely rest and roost in the huge spreading canopy; a single mature tree can host 5–20 large-bird roosting visits per day. [Portland.gov Pterocarya](https://www.portland.gov/trees/pterocarya-fraxinifolia)
- **Eurasian Jay (Garrulus glandarius)** — 1–few birds in autumn raid the long nutlet strings; Juglandaceae seeds are favoured corvid food. [Botanical Realm Pterocarya](https://www.botanicalrealm.com/plant-identification/caucasian-wingnut-pterocarya-fraxinifolia/)
- **Squirrels (Sciurus vulgaris / S. carolinensis)** — 1–2 squirrels per mature riverside tree caching the winged nutlets; small Juglandaceae nuts are workable for them despite the wings. [Botanical Realm Pterocarya](https://www.botanicalrealm.com/plant-identification/caucasian-wingnut-pterocarya-fraxinifolia/)
- **Cavity-nesting birds & roosting bats (Parus, Pipistrellus)** — large old wingnuts develop generous trunk cavities; typically 1–2 bird pairs and occasional bat roosts per veteran tree (qualitative). [NTS plant of the month](https://www.nts.org.uk/stories/plant-of-the-month-pterocarya-fraxinifolia)
- **Roe deer browsing (Capreolus capreolus)** — single deer browse young suckers from the aggressive root-sucker thickets along Amsterdam waterways; one of the tree's main mammal interactions. [NTS plant of the month](https://www.nts.org.uk/stories/plant-of-the-month-pterocarya-fraxinifolia)
