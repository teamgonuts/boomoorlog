# Catalpa — Catalpa (Catalpa)

**Archetype (draft):** Support
**Amsterdam trees:** 700

## Personality
The flamboyant showboat of the boulevard — enormous heart-shaped leaves, orchid-like flowers, absurdly long seed pods dangling like ammunition belts, and poisonous roots that dare anything to dig too close.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 12–18 m typical; up to 27 m (Northern Catalpa, C. speciosa)
- **Lifespan:** 50–70 years; relatively short-lived, trunks prone to internal rot on older specimens
- **Wood:** Air-dry density ~460 kg/m³; Janka hardness ~2,450 N (550 lbf) — light, soft, and low-density; very rot-resistant despite softness (used historically for fence posts and railroad ties)
- **Growth speed:** Fast (rapid early growth, slows with age)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Native to eastern North America and East Asia; the catalpaworm (Ceratomia catalpae) larvae feed exclusively on its leaves — the tree is sometimes deliberately grown as fish-bait "worm farms"; roots are highly poisonous; aerial parts (leaves, flowers, pods) are non-toxic to humans and pets; large flowers attract bumblebees; somewhat invasive outside native range; culturally prominent street tree worldwide; produces extremely long (20–50 cm) cigar-like seed pods

## Combat flavor
Light wood delivers rapid strikes, but don't underestimate it — its toxic roots could trigger a "ground poison" area-denial ability, and the sheer leaf canopy size (natural umbrella) makes it a natural protector/buffer Support unit for adjacent allies.

## Living creatures
Catalpa is a non-native ornamental in Europe — its associated fauna is sparse, dominated by big-bee pollinators on the trumpet flowers and a handful of generalist pests; the famous catalpa sphinx and predator-recruiting nectar deterrents are mostly a North-American phenomenon.

- **Aphids (Aphidoidea, generalist spp.)** — hundreds to low thousands per tree at peak; routinely listed as one of the few real Catalpa pests. [UKy Entomology: Catalpa pests](https://www.uky.edu/Ag/Entomology/treepestguide/catalpa.html)
- **Mealybugs (Pseudococcidae)** — dozens to hundreds per infested tree; cottony masses on twigs causing leaf-drop. [UKy Entomology: Catalpa pests](https://www.uky.edu/Ag/Entomology/treepestguide/catalpa.html)
- **Verticillium wilt-associated fungi (Verticillium spp.)** — soil-borne, not an animal, but the dominant biotic interaction for older Catalpa; included for completeness. [UKy Entomology: Catalpa pests](https://www.uky.edu/Ag/Entomology/treepestguide/catalpa.html)
- **Honeybees (Apis mellifera)** — 50–200+ workers actively foraging a single tree at peak bloom; "abundant nectar in bell-shaped flowers large enough for bees to crawl into." [Bee Happy Plants: Catalpa bignonioides](https://beehappyplants.co.uk/bee-plants/catalpa-bignonioides-2/)
- **Bumblebees (Bombus spp., both short- and long-tongued)** — 10–40 foragers per tree at peak; primary pollinators in NW Europe given absence of hummingbirds. [Bee Happy Plants: Catalpa bignonioides](https://beehappyplants.co.uk/bee-plants/catalpa-bignonioides-2/)
- **Violet carpenter bee (Xylocopa violacea)** — 0–several per tree where range overlaps; expanding northward in Europe and large enough to fully enter the flower. [Wikipedia: Xylocopa violacea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylocopa_violacea)
- **Catalpa sphinx (Ceratomia catalpae)** — host-specific defoliator, abundant in N. America (dozens of caterpillars per tree); essentially absent on European trees — flagged honestly as a missing piece. [Texas A&M: Catalpa Sphinx](https://texasinsects.tamu.edu/catalpa-sphinx/)
- **Night-flying hawkmoths (Sphingidae, e.g. Macroglossum stellatarum)** — handful per evening visiting the pale, fragrant flowers; documented Catalpa nectar visitors. [Northern Catalpa SBVPA writeup](https://sbvpa.org/treetrail/northern-catalpa/)
- **Roosting birds (House Sparrow Passer domesticus, Wood Pigeon Columba palumbus)** — 1–5 individuals using the huge umbrella canopy for shelter; dense foliage provides excellent cover. [Northern Catalpa SBVPA writeup](https://sbvpa.org/treetrail/northern-catalpa/)
- **Nesting tits (Cyanistes caeruleus / Parus major)** — 0–1 pairs per mature urban Catalpa where cavities form on older rot-prone trunks. [Bat Conservation Trust: roosts in trees](https://www.bats.org.uk/about-bats/where-do-bats-live/bat-roosts/roosts-in-trees)
