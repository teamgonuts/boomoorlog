# Nyssa — Tupelo (Nyssa)

**Archetype (draft):** Skirmisher
**Amsterdam trees:** 204

## Personality
The oldest hardwood fighter in the eastern forest — wiry, interlocked-grained, and maddeningly hard to split; a shadow-tolerant survivor that outlasts foes by simply refusing to fall.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 9–15 m typical; max ~27 m (Nyssa sylvatica)
- **Lifespan:** 500–679 years recorded maximum; longest-lived broadleaf in eastern North America — notably long-lived
- **Wood:** ~530 kg/m³ air-dry density; Janka ~800 lbf — heavy, cross-grained, extremely difficult to split
- **Growth speed:** Medium (slow in later life)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** High shade tolerance; flood-tolerant; interlocked grain resists splitting better than almost any other hardwood; prized honey tree (Tupelo honey); brilliant scarlet autumn colour; low natural rot resistance; rare in Amsterdam (only 204 individuals)

## Combat flavor
The cross-grained, unsplittable wood makes Nyssa a classic skirmisher who absorbs glancing blows and keeps going; its exceptional longevity suggests a late-game endurance bonus. Signature ability candidate: **Grain Lock** — chance to deflect melee hits entirely due to the interlocked wood structure.

## Living creatures
Tupelo (Nyssa) is non-native to Europe and supports a small local fauna here — but its nectar is famously bee-attractive (Tupelo honey) and its sour autumn fruit is a magnet for thrushes.

- **Honey bees (Apis mellifera / Apidae)** — hundreds per flowering tree; the genus is a prime nectar tree, source of commercial Tupelo honey. [Wikipedia: Nyssa sylvatica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyssa_sylvatica)
- **Aphids (Aphidoidea, e.g. Aphis coreopsidis)** — colonies of hundreds to low thousands; one of the few co-evolved sap-feeders. [Illinois Wildflowers: Black Tupelo](https://illinoiswildflowers.info/trees/plants/bl_tupelo.html)
- **Tupelo leaf-miner moth & generalist Lepidoptera** — 5–30 caterpillars per tree in season; 26 Lepidoptera species use Nyssa in its native range, far fewer in Europe. [Wikipedia: Nyssa sylvatica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyssa_sylvatica)
- **Forest tent caterpillar (Malacosoma disstria, native range only)** — dozens to hundreds per tree in outbreaks; absent in Europe. [Illinois Wildflowers: Black Tupelo](https://illinoiswildflowers.info/trees/plants/bl_tupelo.html)
- **Armored scales & mealybugs (Diaspididae, Pseudococcidae)** — hundreds per twig on infested trees; generalist sap-feeders. [Illinois Wildflowers: Black Tupelo](https://illinoiswildflowers.info/trees/plants/bl_tupelo.html)
- **Blackbirds & thrushes (Turdus, including winter migrants)** — 3–10 individuals on a fruiting tree; the sour drupes are a key autumn-migration fuel. [Wikipedia: Nyssa sylvatica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyssa_sylvatica)
- **Starlings (Sturnus vulgaris)** — flocks of 10–50 stripping a fruiting tree; the noisy autumn presence. [Backyard Ecology: Black gum](https://www.backyardecology.net/black-gum-a-wonderful-wildlife-tree-that-also-works-well-in-landscaped-settings/)
- **Wood pigeons & corvids (Columba/Corvus)** — 1–3 individuals per tree; eat the fruit and use the crown for nesting. [Backyard Ecology: Black gum](https://www.backyardecology.net/black-gum-a-wonderful-wildlife-tree-that-also-works-well-in-landscaped-settings/)
- **Squirrels & opossums (small mammals)** — 1–3 individuals; older Nyssa develop trunk hollows ideal for dens. [Wikipedia: Nyssa sylvatica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyssa_sylvatica)
- **Cavity-roosting bats (Pipistrellus spp.) & feral honeybee colonies** — single roosts in older hollow trunks; Nyssa is famously cavity-rich with age. [Wikipedia: Nyssa sylvatica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyssa_sylvatica)
