# Corylus — Hazel (Corylus)

**Archetype (draft):** Support
**Amsterdam trees:** 1247

## Personality
A scrappy, unkillable underdog who keeps coming back stronger every time you cut her down — coppicing is not death, it's a power move.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 3–6 m typical (shrub/multi-stem); Turkish hazel (C. colurna) up to 35 m
- **Lifespan:** 70–80 years uncoppiced; potentially several hundred years when coppiced regularly
- **Wood:** ~517 kg/m³ air-dry density — comparable to pine/spruce, relatively light and flexible
- **Growth speed:** medium (30–60 cm/yr)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Coppice champion — regenerates vigorously from cut stump; mast crop (hazelnuts) critical for dormice, squirrels, and birds; important early-spring pollen source; no thorns; traditional coppice cycles of 7–15 years; common across European woodland understory

## Combat flavor
Hazel's coppice resilience is the defining mechanic hook — a low-HP support unit that regenerates hitpoints or revives after defeat, weaker wood making it fast but not heavy-hitting. Nut-drop could function as an area food-buff for allied units.

## Living creatures
Hazel is a wildlife heavyweight — over 250 associated insect species plus the iconic hazel-dormouse-and-nut food web.
- **Aphids (Aphidoidea, esp. Corylobium avellanae, Myzocallis coryli)** — ~1000s per tree across the canopy; hazel hosts several specialist aphids on young shoots and leaves. [Aphids on hazel](https://influentialpoints.com/Gallery/Aphids_on_hazel_Corylus_avellana.htm)
- **Gall mites (Phytoptus avellanae, big bud mite)** — ~100s of swollen buds per tree in infested specimens; conspicuous "big bud" galls. [RHS hazel big bud mite](https://www.rhs.org.uk/biodiversity/hazel-big-bud-mite)
- **Moth caterpillars — Nut-tree Tussock, Large Emerald, Barred Umber, Small White Wave (Lepidoptera)** — ~10–100 per mature tree in spring; classic hazel-feeding macromoths. [Woodland Trust hazel](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hazel/)
- **Hazel sawflies (Tenthredinidae, e.g. Croesus septentrionalis)** — ~5–30 larvae per branch when present; gregarious S-shaped feeders. [Hazel wildlife profile](https://www.shropshirewildlifetrust.org.uk/wildlife-explorer/trees-and-shrubs/hazel)
- **Nut weevil (Curculio nucum)** — ~5–40% of nuts grub-infested per tree; larva develops inside a single hazelnut. [Curculio nucum overview](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curculio_nucum)
- **Honeybees & early bumblebees (Apidae)** — ~100s of catkin visits per tree on warm February days; hazel is a critical early pollen source. [Hazel as early pollen source](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hazel/)
- **Blue & Great Tits (Cyanistes caeruleus / Parus major)** — 1–2 nesting pairs in a coppice clump; gleaning caterpillars and using cavities. [Woodland Trust hazel wildlife](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hazel/)
- **Nuthatch & Great Spotted Woodpecker (Sitta europaea / Dendrocopos major)** — 1–2 individuals per fruiting tree in autumn, hammering nuts into bark crevices. [Woodland Trust nuthatch](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/animals/birds/nuthatch/)
- **Hazel Dormouse (Muscardinus avellanarius)** — 0–1 per tree in good habitat (rare in Amsterdam itself); the textbook hazel mammal, fattening on nuts for hibernation. [Woodland Trust hazel dormouse](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/animals/mammals/hazel-dormouse/)
- **Wood Mouse / Bank Vole / Red Squirrel (Apodemus sylvaticus / Myodes glareolus / Sciurus vulgaris)** — 2–6 small mammals working a fruiting tree in autumn, leaving diagnostic gnaw-marks on nutshells. [Hazel wildlife champion](https://wyevalehedges.co.uk/blogs/blog/hazel-trees-a-wildlife)
