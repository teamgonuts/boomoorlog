# Ulmus — Elm (Iep)

**Archetype (draft):** Bruiser
**Amsterdam trees:** 37505

## Personality
Amsterdam's most common street warrior — scarred by plague, stubbornly resilient, hitting harder than anyone expects from a tree that should have died decades ago.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 21–35 m; up to ~40 m in ideal conditions
- **Lifespan:** Typically 200 years; some specimens exceed 300 years; European field populations dramatically reduced by Dutch elm disease (Ophiostoma novo-ulmi)
- **Wood:** ~550 kg/m³ (air-dry); interlocked grain makes it very tough and resistant to splitting; soft/rock elm species range 560–660 kg/m³; Janka ~800–1320 lbf depending on species
- **Growth speed:** Fast (3–6 feet/year in ideal conditions)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Highly tolerant of wet, compacted, and urban soils; interlocked grain historically prized for wheel hubs and coffins precisely because it will not split under impact; devastated across Europe and North America by Dutch elm disease spread by bark beetles; resistant cultivars now deployed widely; no significant toxicity or allelopathy; keystone riparian species

## Combat flavor
Fast growth means quick leveling and rapid deployment; interlocked grain gives exceptional toughness (hard to one-shot), and the scars of Dutch elm disease could manifest as a unique plague-resistance or disease-immunity mechanic.

## Living creatures
Elm (Ulmus) was once the keystone deciduous host in NW Europe — Kennedy & Southwood ranked it among the very richest British trees for insect species, and the White-letter Hairstreak butterfly lives nowhere else. Dutch elm disease gutted the network but Amsterdam's surviving elm stock (the largest in Europe) still carries that ecological weight.

- **Elm leaf aphid (Tinocallis platani, Eriosoma spp., Aphididae)** — multiple aphid species, including the gall-forming Eriosoma ulmi making the leaf-curl "purse galls"; populations in the tens of thousands per tree. [Woodland Trust: Wych Elm](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/wych-elm/)
- **White-letter Hairstreak (Satyrium w-album, Lycaenidae)** — the obligate elm butterfly; ~1–20 individuals per mature elm in NW Europe, almost the entire population sits on this genus. [Butterfly Conservation: White-letter Hairstreak](https://butterfly-conservation.org/butterflies/white-letter-hairstreak)
- **White-spotted Pinion moth (Cosmia diffinis, Noctuidae)** — UK BAP priority moth, elm specialist; ~1–10 larvae per tree where it survives. [Woodland Trust: Wych Elm](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/wych-elm/)
- **Peppered & Light Emerald moths (Biston betularia, Campaea margaritaria)** plus other generalist Geometridae — dozens to low hundreds of caterpillars per tree. [Woodland Trust: Wych Elm](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/wych-elm/)
- **Elm bark beetles (Scolytus scolytus, S. multistriatus, Curculionidae: Scolytinae)** — the Dutch elm disease vectors; tens to hundreds breeding under the bark of every weakened tree. [Wikipedia: Dutch elm disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dutch_elm_disease)
- **Bullfinch (Pyrrhula pyrrhula)** — a signature elm-seed feeder in spring; small flocks of 2–10 birds per fruiting tree. [Woodland Trust: Wych Elm](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/wych-elm/)
- **Blue & Great Tits (Cyanistes caeruleus / Parus major)** — heavy caterpillar harvest from elms; 2–4 pairs per mature tree. [Woodland Trust: Field Elm](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/field-elm/)
- **Goldfinch & Greenfinch (Carduelis carduelis / Chloris chloris)** — flocks stripping the early samaras in March–April; 5–30 birds per tree. [Woodland Trust: Wych Elm](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/wych-elm/)
- **Bark-fissure mites & springtails (Acari + Collembola)** — sheer count winner: easily 10,000+ per mature trunk. [Trees for Life: Wych Elm](https://treesforlife.org.uk/into-the-forest/trees-plants-animals/trees/elm/wych-elm-facts/)
- **Grey Squirrel / Red Squirrel (Sciurus carolinensis / S. vulgaris)** — building dreys in the crown, gnawing buds and bark; 1–2 per mature tree. [Trees for Life: Wych Elm](https://treesforlife.org.uk/into-the-forest/trees-plants-animals/trees/elm/wych-elm-facts/)
