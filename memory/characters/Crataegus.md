# Crataegus — Hawthorn (Meidoorn)

**Archetype (draft):** Support
**Amsterdam trees:** 5809

## Personality
A battle-scarred hedgerow veteran bristling with thorns, it fights dirty with a laugh — the original barbed-wire barrier, ancient enough to have watched empires rise and fall from behind its spiky ramparts.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 5–10 m typical; up to 14 m in favourable conditions
- **Lifespan:** 250 years typical; up to 400 years on good sites — notably long-lived for a small tree
- **Wood:** ~865 kg/m³ air-dry (based on Crataegus mexicana; genus average similar — dense, tough hardwood); name literally derives from Greek for "hardness"
- **Growth speed:** Slow to medium
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Dense, multi-branched thorns up to 10 cm long — classic defensive barrier plant; berries (haws) feed birds and small mammals; important nectar source for pollinators; highly adaptable to poor and dry soils; culturally revered in Celtic tradition as a fairy tree; used in herbal medicine for the heart

## Combat flavor
Dense, slow-growing, and armored — Crataegus is a defensive specialist whose thorns make melee attackers think twice. Its long lifespan hints at a passive regeneration or fortification ability: old hedges that just won't die.

## Living creatures
Hawthorn supports over 300 insect species and is the prototype "wildlife hedge" — nectar in May, haws all winter, and dense thorn-armoured nest sites in between.
- **Aphids (Aphidoidea, esp. Dysaphis crataegi, Ovatus crataegarius)** — ~1000s per tree on young shoots; multiple specialist hawthorn aphids. [Aphids on hawthorn](https://influentialpoints.com/Gallery/Aphids_on_hawthorn_Crataegus_in_Britain.htm)
- **Hawthorn moth caterpillars (Lepidoptera, incl. Trichiura crataegi, lackey, vapourer, light emerald)** — ~100s of larvae per mature tree in spring; 9+ named hawthorn-feeding moths. [Woodland Trust hawthorn](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hawthorn/)
- **Hawthorn shieldbug (Acanthosoma haemorrhoidale)** — ~5–30 adults & nymphs per fruiting tree; signature haw-feeder. [Acanthosoma haemorrhoidale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acanthosoma_haemorrhoidale)
- **Pollinators on Mayflower (Diptera, Hymenoptera, Coleoptera)** — ~100s of visits per tree per day during the May bloom; hoverflies, solitary bees, beetles. [Crataegus monogyna ecology](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1365-2745.13541)
- **Hawthorn leaf beetle (Lochmaea crataegi)** — ~10–100 per tree in season; specialist hawthorn chrysomelid. [Lochmaea crataegi genome](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11380073/)
- **Gall mites (Eriophyes spp.)** — ~100s of leaf-rolls and warts per tree; multiple hawthorn-specific eriophyids. [Crataegus pests](https://www.gardenia.net/guide/crataegus-hawthorn-pests-and-diseases)
- **Blue & Great Tits, Long-tailed Tit (Paridae / Aegithalidae)** — 1–3 nesting pairs in or beside a mature thorny tree; thorns deter predators. [Hawthorn nesting cover](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hawthorn/)
- **Redwing & Fieldfare (Turdus iliacus / pilaris)** — 5–50 birds per fruiting tree in winter; haws are a staple of migrant thrushes. [Woodland Trust hawthorn wildlife](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hawthorn/)
- **Wood mouse / Bank vole (Apodemus sylvaticus / Myodes glareolus)** — 2–8 individuals per tree caching haw stones under leaf litter. [Crataegus monogyna ecology](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1365-2745.13541)
- **Hazel Dormouse (Muscardinus avellanarius)** — 0–1 per tree (rare in Amsterdam) eating hawthorn flowers in spring and haws in autumn. [Hawthorn dormouse food](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hawthorn/)
