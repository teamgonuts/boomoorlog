# Cercis — Redbud (Cercis)

**Archetype (draft):** Support
**Amsterdam trees:** 228

## Personality
A nitrogen-fixing pacifist who feeds the soil around every ally, then erupts in shocking pink flowers straight out of bare wood — no leaves needed, just drama.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 6–12 m; typically a small spreading tree
- **Lifespan:** 50–70 years (some sources cite 20–25 years for stressed urban specimens; healthy trees reach 70)
- **Wood:** Hard, finely grained, beautifully veined — takes a high polish; used for decorative veneers. No reliable kg/m³ figure widely published; Janka for C. canadensis ~830 lbf (estimated from comparable legume hardwoods)
- **Growth speed:** fast when young (30–45 cm/yr), slows with age — overall medium
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Legume family (Fabaceae) — fixes atmospheric nitrogen, enriching surrounding soil; cauliflorous flowering (blooms erupt directly from trunk and branches, not just twigs); no thorns; tolerates poor, alkaline, and clay soils; native range southern Europe and SW Asia; culturally significant (Judas tree legend); attracts bees and early pollinators; notable but not globally rare

## Combat flavor
Nitrogen-fixing makes Cercis the premier soil-buffing support — a passive aura that boosts growth speed or HP regeneration of adjacent allies. Cauliflory (trunk-flowering) is visually bizarre enough to become a signature ability: blooms burst from its own bark mid-battle.

## Living creatures
Cercis is a small but ecologically loud tree — a Fabaceae nitrogen-fixer whose early-spring cauliflorous bloom is a magnet for solitary bees, paired with a host-specific Asian seed beetle (Bruchidius siliquastri) now invading European cities.

- **Judas tree seed beetle (Bruchidius siliquastri)** — hundreds to low thousands of seeds attacked per mature tree; recorded infestation rates of 75–82% of seed pods. [Forestpests.eu: Bruchidius siliquastri](https://www.forestpests.eu/pest/bruchidius-siliquastri)
- **Aphids (Aphidoidea, generalist spp.)** — hundreds to low thousands per tree at peak; standard early-spring legume colonisers. [NC Extension: Cercis siliquastrum](https://plants.ces.ncsu.edu/plants/cercis-siliquastrum/)
- **Honeybees (Apis mellifera)** — 50–200 workers per tree during the few-week spring bloom; RHS Plant for Pollinators. [NC Extension: Cercis siliquastrum](https://plants.ces.ncsu.edu/plants/cercis-siliquastrum/)
- **Bumblebees (Bombus spp.)** — 20–60 workers per tree; visit the pea-flowers for nectar and pollen. [Capital Naturalist: Eastern Redbud](http://capitalnaturalist.blogspot.com/2014/04/the-judas-tree.html)
- **Mason and mining bees (Osmia, Andrena spp.)** — dozens per tree at bloom peak; key early-spring pollen source for solitary bees. [Backyard Ecology: Eastern redbud](https://www.backyardecology.net/eastern-redbud/)
- **Carpenter bees (Xylocopa spp.)** — 0–several per tree where range overlaps; visit and nectar-rob the larger flowers. [Capital Naturalist: Eastern Redbud](http://capitalnaturalist.blogspot.com/2014/04/the-judas-tree.html)
- **Leafcutter bees (Megachile spp.)** — dozens per tree; preferentially cut neat circles from redbud leaves to line tube nests. [Capital Naturalist: Eastern Redbud](http://capitalnaturalist.blogspot.com/2014/04/the-judas-tree.html)
- **Redbud leaf-folder moth and Lepidoptera larvae (Fascista cercerisella and ~10–19 spp.)** — dozens of caterpillars per tree in N. America; far fewer on European Cercis siliquastrum but documented. [Backyard Ecology: Eastern redbud](https://www.backyardecology.net/eastern-redbud/)
- **Rhizobial root bacteria (Bradyrhizobium / Rhizobium nodules)** — millions per tree; not animals but the load-bearing symbiosis that defines Cercis ecologically. [NC Extension: Cercis canadensis](https://plants.ces.ncsu.edu/plants/cercis-canadensis/)
- **Tits and small passerines (Cyanistes caeruleus, Parus major, Sylvia atricapilla)** — 1–2 individuals regularly working a mature tree, gleaning aphids; usually too small for nesting. [Bat Conservation Trust: roosts in trees](https://www.bats.org.uk/about-bats/where-do-bats-live/bat-roosts/roosts-in-trees)
