# Malus — Apple (Appel)

**Archetype (draft):** Support
**Amsterdam trees:** 4344

## Personality
The orchard patriarch — tough, productive, and stubbornly moderate, with wood harder than walnut and a cultural footprint spanning every temperate civilization on Earth.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 5–10 m cultivated; standard trees up to 12 m; wild M. sylvestris up to 15 m
- **Lifespan:** 25–100 years for cultivated varieties; espaliered specimens productive for 150+ yr; wild trees up to 100 yr
- **Wood:** specific gravity 0.73–0.83 (approx. 780 kg/m³ air-dry); Janka ~7,700 N (1,730 lbf) — harder than cherry and walnut, comparable to hickory
- **Growth speed:** medium
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Malus sylvestris (wild crab apple) is a light-demanding pioneer species and notably rare in native European range; ancestor of all domestic apples is Malus sieversii from Central Asian mountains; supports diverse insects, birds, and mammals; no allelopathy; some crab-apple species have mild seed toxicity (amygdalin/cyanogenic glycosides in seeds — classic lore); deeply embedded in human mythology (Norse, Greek, Arthurian)

## Combat flavor
Malus fights with measured, heavy precision — its dense, hickory-comparable wood drives slow powerful attacks — while its mythological weight (forbidden fruit, golden apples) hints at a "Temptation" debuff ability that lures enemy units out of formation.

## Living creatures
Apple (Malus) is one of Europe's richest insect hosts — ~200+ phytophagous arthropod species recorded in European orchards alone (Kennedy & Southwood-style counts), plus a heavy mammal and bird load from fallen fruit.

- **Rosy apple aphid & green apple aphid (Dysaphis plantaginea, Aphis pomi / Aphididae)** — thousands per tree in spring colonies; the dominant sap-feeders. [bioRxiv: parasitoid control of D. plantaginea](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2021.09.20.461076.full.pdf)
- **Codling moth (Cydia pomonella / Tortricidae)** — typically 10–50 caterpillars per untreated tree per generation; the textbook "worm in the apple". [RHS: Codling moth](https://www.rhs.org.uk/biodiversity/codling-moth)
- **Winter moth caterpillars (Operophtera brumata / Geometridae)** — hundreds per tree in spring outbreaks; defoliators that feed blue tit chicks. [RHS: Winter moth caterpillars](https://www.rhs.org.uk/biodiversity/winter-moth-caterpillars)
- **Apple blossom weevil (Anthonomus pomorum / Curculionidae)** — dozens to hundreds per tree; classic spring florivore on Malus sylvestris and domestica. [PMC: Anthonomus pomorum on Malus](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9291012/)
- **Eyed hawk-moth, pale tussock, green pug, Chinese character (various Lepidoptera)** — 5–30 caterpillars in season; leaf-feeders that overlap with many other hosts. [Woodland Trust: Crab apple](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/crab-apple/)
- **Honey bees & solitary bees (Apis mellifera, Andrena spp. / Apidae+Andrenidae)** — hundreds foraging at peak bloom; apple blossom is one of the top early-season nectar resources. [Wildlife Trusts: Crab apple](https://www.wildlifetrusts.org/wildlife-explorer/trees-and-shrubs/crab-apple)
- **Blackbirds & thrushes (Turdus merula, T. philomelos / Turdidae)** — 1–5 individuals per fruiting tree; aggressively defend windfalls into winter. [Birdfact: Do birds eat apples](https://www.birdfact.com/articles/do-birds-eat-apples)
- **Fieldfare & redwing (Turdus pilaris, T. iliacus)** — flocks of 10–100+ on a heavily fruiting tree in winter; the iconic orchard scene in NW Europe. [PTES: Orchard biodiversity](https://ptes.org/campaigns/traditional-orchard-project/orchard-biodiversity/)
- **Blue & Great Tits (Cyanistes/Parus)** — 1–2 nesting pairs typical in older trees; their chick-rearing is synchronised with winter-moth caterpillar peaks. [PLOS One: Bird use of organic apple orchards](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0183405)
- **Mice, voles, foxes, badgers (various mammals)** — single individuals each, regular visitors; eat windfalls and disperse seed. [Woodland Trust: Crab apple](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/crab-apple/)
