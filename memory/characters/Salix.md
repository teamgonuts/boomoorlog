# Salix — Willow (Wilg)

**Archetype (draft):** Juggernaut
**Amsterdam trees:** 13 546

## Personality
A restless, water-logged brawler who grows faster than anyone expects and hits so relentlessly — with light, whippy wood — that enemies drown in sheer volume of blows before they can react.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 10–25 m typical; some species reach 30 m
- **Lifespan:** 30–75 years (short for its size; reproduces vegetatively to persist longer)
- **Wood:** ~420–500 kg/m³ air-dry (Salix alba ~420; broader genus average ~500); soft, light, straight-grained — shock-absorbent rather than brittle
- **Growth speed:** Fast (up to 3 m/yr in youth)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Dominates wetlands and riverbanks; roots aggressively stabilise soil and absorb heavy metals / pollutants (phytoremediation); extremely flood-tolerant; short-lived wood regenerates rapidly from coppice; historically used for cricket bats — flexible under impact, not splintering

## Combat flavor
Light, fast wood means rapid-fire strikes rather than crushing blows — Salix is the Juggernaut that overwhelms through relentless speed and biomass rather than raw power. Phytoremediation roots could translate into a passive "poison drain" that clears ground debuffs beneath it.

## Living creatures
Salix is the runner-up to oak for British insect richness — Kennedy & Southwood list ~450 phytophagous invertebrates on willow, including ~160 Lepidoptera and ~100 sawflies/hymenopterans; in early spring it is the single most important nectar source in NW Europe.

- **Aphids (Aphidoidea — Tuberolachnus salignus, Chaitophorus spp.)** — the giant willow aphid (T. salignus) forms colonies of hundreds to many thousands per branch on a mature tree, dripping honeydew. [Buglife giant willow aphid](https://www.buglife.org.uk/bugs/bug-directory/giant-willow-aphid/)
- **Andrena mining bees (Andrena spp., ~10+ Salix-specialist species)** — a mature flowering sallow draws hundreds to low thousands of solitary bee visits per day in March–April. [Scottish Pollinators on willow](https://scottishpollinators.wordpress.com/2021/12/20/the-whomping-willow-and-golden-pollen/)
- **Honey & bumblebees (Apis mellifera / Bombus spp.)** — a single mature catkin-bearing willow can be worked by thousands of bees per day, including emerging Bombus queens. [Dave Cushman on willow pollen](http://www.dave-cushman.net/bee/pollenthesis.html)
- **Willow weevils (Curculionoidea — ~100+ Salix-associated spp. across Europe)** — dozens to hundreds per tree across multiple species; Salix has the richest weevil fauna of any European tree. [Salix transect of Europe weevils](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7286951/)
- **Sawflies (Nematinae / Pontania gallers)** — hundreds of bean-gall and pea-gall structures per tree from Pontania spp.; >70 sawfly species recorded on Salix in Britain. [Woodland Trust goat willow](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/goat-willow/)
- **Orthosia moths (Noctuidae — Common Quaker, Hebrew Character, etc.)** — hundreds of adults per night feeding at catkins in March; key spring biomass for bats. [Trophic Salix-Orthosia-barbastelle](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10163055/)
- **Eyed Hawk-moth & Puss Moth caterpillars (Smerinthus ocellata / Cerura vinula)** — handfuls of larvae per tree, but each is heavyweight biomass (5+ g pre-pupation). [Butterfly Conservation eyed hawk-moth](https://butterfly-conservation.org/moths/eyed-hawk-moth)
- **Purple Emperor & White Admiral (Apatura iris / Limenitis camilla)** — 1 to a few breeding individuals per large sallow in suitable woodland; A. iris obligately uses Salix caprea/cinerea. [Butterfly Conservation purple emperor](https://butterfly-conservation.org/butterflies/purple-emperor)
- **Goat moth larvae (Cossus cossus)** — 1 to a few present in heartwood of an old riverside willow over their 3–5 year larval phase; rare but enormous (10 cm) and structurally damaging. [Wildlife Trusts goat moth](https://www.wildlifetrusts.org/wildlife-explorer/invertebrates/moths/goat-moth)
- **Barbastelle & Pipistrelle bats (Barbastella barbastellus / Pipistrellus spp.)** — 1 to several individuals roost in old willow cavities and forage on the Orthosia swarms; veteran pollards are key bat trees. [Salix-Orthosia-barbastelle study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10163055/)
