# Pyrus — Pear (Peer)

**Archetype (draft):** Support
**Amsterdam trees:** 3065

## Personality
A deceptively tough old-timer who looks like a pastoral fruit-bearer but fights with dense, smooth-grained wood that has outlasted empires — the oldest known individual clocked 458 years.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 10–15 m typical; up to 20 m in wild specimens; max recorded ~25 m
- **Lifespan:** 50–250 years for Pyrus communis; ornamental cultivars (P. calleryana) only 15–25 yr; wild trees can reach 458 yr (record)
- **Wood:** ~690 kg/m³ air-dry; Janka ~7,380 N — harder than oak, fine-grained and dense
- **Growth speed:** medium (ornamentals fast, fruiting trees medium)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Flowers early in spring, providing critical early nectar; P. calleryana is invasive in eastern North America; wood historically prized for precision instruments and woodblock printing; low natural rot resistance; no thorns on cultivated forms (wild P. pyraster has thorns)

## Combat flavor
Dense, smooth pearwood delivers heavy, precise blows — slow wind-up, high impact — while early-blooming flowers give nearby allies a spring-buff of nectar energy. Wild-type thorn variants could unlock a "Bramble Spike" passive.

## Living creatures
As a Rosaceae fruit tree, Pyrus shares much of the rich apple/pear pest-and-pollinator complex — strong early-spring nectar value, codling-moth and psylla as headline specialists, plus old wild-pear trunks as cavity habitat for hole-nesters and bats.

- **Pear psylla (Cacopsylla pyri / C. pyricola)** — the defining pear specialist; populations of tens of thousands per mature tree at peak summer, generating sooty-mould honeydew. [WSU pear psylla IPM](https://treefruit.wsu.edu/crop-protection/opm/pear-psylla/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/cacopsylla-pyri.jpg)
- **Pear & rosy apple aphids (Dysaphis pyri, D. plantaginea)** — leaf-curl colonies of hundreds per shoot, thousands per tree. [PNW pear aphid](https://pnwhandbooks.org/insect/tree-fruit/pear/pear-aphid) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/dysaphis-pyri.jpg)
- **Honey & solitary bees (Apis mellifera, Osmia bicornis, Andrena spp.)** — a mature pear in full bloom attracts thousands of bee visits per day; pear flowers offer thin nectar but huge pollen reward. [Metschnikowia–pear pollinator study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8061982/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/apis-mellifera.jpg)
- **Codling moth (Cydia pomonella)** — dozens to a few hundred larvae per fruiting tree; primary worm-in-the-pear. [PNW codling moth on pear](https://pnwhandbooks.org/insect/tree-fruit/pear/pear-codling-moth) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/cydia-pomonella.jpg)
- **Pear leaf-curling midge (Dasineura pyri)** — hundreds of curled-leaf galls per young tree in spring. [ATTRA pear insect pests](https://attra.ncat.org/publication/pear-insect-pests/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/dasineura-pyri.jpg)
- **Winter moth & tortrix caterpillars (Operophtera brumata, Archips spp.)** — thousands per mature tree in May, shared with the whole rosaceous fruit guild. [UMass winter moth](https://www.umass.edu/agriculture-food-environment/fruit/news/winter-moth-operophtera-brumata) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/operophtera-brumata.jpg)
- **European earwig (Forficula auricularia)** — hundreds per tree under bark scales; key generalist predator of psylla and aphids. [UC IPM pear psylla](https://ipm.ucanr.edu/agriculture/pear/pear-psylla/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/forficula-auricularia.jpg)
- **Blue & Great Tits (Cyanistes caeruleus / Parus major)** — 1–2 nesting pairs per mature orchard pear; old wild-pear cavities are favoured nest sites. [Woodland Trust wildlife trees](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/blog/2023/05/best-trees-for-birds-and-other-wildlife/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/cyanistes-caeruleus.jpg)
- **Frugivorous mammals (Vulpes vulpes, Meles meles, Sus scrofa)** — fox, badger and wild boar individually clear hundreds of fallen pears per night under a fruiting wild-pear. [EUFORGEN Pyrus pyraster](https://www.euforgen.org/species/pyrus-pyraster) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/vulpes-vulpes.jpg)
- **Roosting bats (Pipistrellus / Myotis)** — old wild-pear trunks with rot cavities commonly host 1 to a few roosting bats; documented at orchard scale for noctule and brown long-eared. [Woodland Trust wildlife trees](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/blog/2023/05/best-trees-for-birds-and-other-wildlife/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/pipistrellus.jpg)
