# Liriodendron — Tulip tree (Liriodendron)

**Archetype (draft):** Bruiser
**Amsterdam trees:** 781

## Personality
The tallest hardwood in North America rockets skyward like a living siege tower — column-straight, absurdly fast, and covered in flowers that look like it's already celebrating victory.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 20–30 m in urban settings; 50–60 m in native Appalachian cove forests; exceptional specimens approach 58 m
- **Lifespan:** 250–300 years typical; maximum around 450–500 years
- **Wood:** ~455 kg/m³ (air-dry); Janka ~540 lbf; classified as a hardwood but one of the lightest and softest — workable, straight-grained; commercially sold as "yellow poplar"
- **Growth speed:** Fast (60–90 cm/year, up to 1.2 m in ideal conditions)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Living fossil — genus dates to the Cretaceous; only two living species (L. tulipifera in North America, L. chinense in China), a classic Gondwana disjunct distribution; large, nectar-rich tulip-shaped flowers are a major pollinator resource; straight trunk with no lower branching gives extraordinary height-to-base ratio; intolerant of waterlogged soil; fast growth without the typical brittle-wood penalty; state tree of three US states

## Combat flavor
Light wood means fast attack speed despite its massive size — a Bruiser that trades armor for reach and speed, and its living-fossil status could unlock a "ancient lineage" legendary-tier ability.

## Living creatures
Tulip tree (Liriodendron) is non-native to Europe, so its associated invertebrate fauna is small — but its nectar-rich flowers make it a heavyweight resource for generalist pollinators.

- **Tulip-tree aphid (Illinoia liriodendri / Aphididae)** — thousands per tree in summer; the dominant herbivore, monophagous on Liriodendron, now widespread across Europe. [EPPO global pest record](https://gd.eppo.int/taxon/LIRTU/pests) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/illinoia-liriodendri.jpg)
- **Tulip-tree scale (Toumeyella liriodendri / Coccidae)** — hundreds to thousands per tree on twigs; produces copious honeydew. [NC State Extension on tulip-tree honeydew](https://entomology.ces.ncsu.edu/news/tulip-tree-aphids-and-scales-feeding-and-dropping-honeydew/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/toumeyella-liriodendri.jpg)
- **Honey bees (Apis mellifera / Apidae)** — dozens to hundreds foraging simultaneously on a flowering tree; one of the heaviest nectar producers a single tree can offer in late spring. [Backyard Ecology — tulip poplars for pollinators](https://www.backyardecology.net/tulip-poplars-a-source-of-abundant-nectar-and-pollen-for-kentucky-pollinators/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/apis-mellifera.jpg)
- **Bumblebees (Bombus spp. / Apidae)** — 10–50 individuals on a flowering canopy; deep cup-shaped flowers favour long-tongued visitors. [Backyard Ecology](https://www.backyardecology.net/tulip-poplars-a-source-of-abundant-nectar-and-pollen-for-kentucky-pollinators/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/bombus.jpg)
- **Honeydew-feeding ants (Formicidae, e.g. Lasius niger)** — hundreds tending aphid colonies; classic mutualism on infested trees. [UMass Amherst aphid guide](https://www.umass.edu/agriculture-food-environment/landscape/publications-resources/insect-mite-guide/illinoia-liriodendri) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/formicidae-e-g-lasius-niger.jpg)
- **Sooty-mould-grazing mites (Acari)** — uncountable on honeydew-coated bark and leaves; not specific to Liriodendron but consistently present. [NC State Extension](https://entomology.ces.ncsu.edu/news/tulip-tree-aphids-and-scales-feeding-and-dropping-honeydew/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/acari.jpg)
- **Wasps (Vespidae) and hoverflies (Syrphidae)** — dozens drawn to honeydew and exposed flower nectar. [NC State Extension](https://entomology.ces.ncsu.edu/news/tulip-tree-aphids-and-scales-feeding-and-dropping-honeydew/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/vespidae.jpg)
- **Blue & Great Tits (Cyanistes/Parus / Paridae)** — usually 1 nesting pair using cavity or fork; gleaning aphids and scale through the season. [Woodland Trust — trees for birds](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/blog/2023/05/best-trees-for-birds-and-other-wildlife/) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/cyanistes-parus.jpg)
- **Grey squirrel (Sciurus carolinensis, where present)** — 1–3 individuals using crown for travel and nest sites; not a major food source. [USDA Forest Service species review](https://research.fs.usda.gov/feis/species-reviews/lirtul) [pic](../../data/creature_pics/sciurus-carolinensis.jpg)
- **Cavity-nesting bats (Pipistrellus spp.)** — occasional 1 roost in older trunk splits; qualitative only, no per-tree count in European literature.
