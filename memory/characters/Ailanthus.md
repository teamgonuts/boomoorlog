# Ailanthus — Tree of Heaven (Ailanthus)

**Archetype (draft):** Juggernaut
**Amsterdam trees:** 885

## Personality
A shameless, unstoppable invader from China who cracks concrete with its roots, poisons the soil around itself, and stinks of victory — because it always comes back no matter what you do to it.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 17–27 m; reaches 15 m in only 25 years
- **Lifespan:** 50 years individual stems; clonal thickets persist far longer via resprouting
- **Wood:** ~600 kg/m³ air-dry (37.1 lb/ft³); Janka hardness ~6 300 N (1 420 lbf) — surprisingly hard and heavy for a fast-growing tree
- **Growth speed:** Fast (one of the fastest-growing trees in the temperate world)
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Allelopathic — exudes ailanthone from roots and bark that suppresses germination of competing plants; rated among the worst invasive species in Europe and North America; tolerates compacted, polluted, and nutrient-poor soils; grows through asphalt and concrete; profuse seed production (up to 350 000 seeds/tree/yr); foliage and male flowers produce a nauseating odour; native to northeast China and Taiwan
- **World rarity:** common (invasive cosmopolitan)

## Combat flavor
Fast growth and surprisingly dense, hard wood makes Ailanthus a deceptive Juggernaut: quick to arrive, heavy in impact, and nearly impossible to eradicate. Allelopathy naturally becomes a ground-denial aura that debuffs or damages enemies standing near its roots.

## Living creatures
Ailanthus is an ecological desert in NW Europe — very few native insects can handle its quassinoid chemistry — but it has a small, distinctive set of specialists and is a known magnet for one looming invasive: the spotted lanternfly.

- **Spotted lanternfly (Lycorma delicatula / Fulgoridae)** — can reach thousands per mature tree where established; Ailanthus is its preferred host and the species is on the EU watchlist for likely arrival. [Bug of the Week: Tree of Heaven & lanternfly](https://bugoftheweek.com/blog/2024/10/7/watch-out-tree-of-heaven-native-and-invasive-insects-are-ready-to-wage-war-on-you-ailanthus-webworm-atteva-aurea-and-spotted-lanternfly-lycorma-delicatula)
- **Ailanthus webworm moth (Atteva aurea / Attevidae)** — dozens of larvae per tree where present; specialist that tracked Ailanthus across the world. [Wikipedia: Ailanthus webworm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ailanthus_webworm)
- **Ailanthus eriophyid mite (Aculus mosoniensis / Eriophyidae)** — easily tens of thousands per tree; the one mite species in Europe associated specifically with Ailanthus and now under study as a possible biocontrol. [PMC: Aculus mosoniensis](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9146616/)
- **Spiny whitefly (Aleurocanthus spiniferus / Aleyrodidae)** — recently recorded on Ailanthus in southern Europe, often hundreds per leaf colony. [PMC: First report Aleurocanthus on Ailanthus](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7142546/)
- **Generalist sap-feeders (assorted Aphidoidea, Coccidae)** — modest numbers; few aphids tolerate the bitter sap, so per-tree counts stay low compared to native trees. [Wikipedia: Ailanthus altissima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ailanthus_altissima)
- **Honey bees & flies at flowering males (Apis mellifera, Calliphoridae)** — dozens, drawn by the foul-smelling male inflorescences which mimic carrion to attract pollinators. [Wikipedia: Ailanthus altissima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ailanthus_altissima)
- **Ants on extrafloral nectaries (Lasius, Formica / Formicidae)** — a column of hundreds is normal; young leaves secrete sugar at the leaflet bases. [Wikipedia: Ailanthus altissima](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ailanthus_altissima)
- **Generalist canopy spiders (Araneidae, Theridiidae)** — a few dozen webs per mature tree, exploiting the dense compound foliage. [Discover Wild Science: Ailanthus](https://discoverwildscience.com/the-tree-that-poisoned-the-prairie-ailanthus-altissima-tree-of-heaven-explained-3-334158/)
- **Opportunistic city birds (Carduelis, Passer)** — 1–2 pairs of urban birds (greenfinch, house sparrow) may nest in the canopy or eat the wind-borne samaras; not a significant food tree. [IUCN GISD: Ailanthus altissima](https://www.iucngisd.org/gisd/species.php?sc=319)
- **Roosting Common Pipistrelle (Pipistrellus pipistrellus)** — 1 bat occasionally; older Ailanthus stems split and host crevice-roosting bats in cities, though no formal counts exist. [Bat Conservation Trust: Bats and woodland](https://www.bats.org.uk/our-work/landscapes-for-bats/bats-and-woodland)
