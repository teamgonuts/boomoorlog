# Carpinus — Hornbeam (Haagbeuk)

**Archetype (draft):** Juggernaut
**Amsterdam trees:** 6879

## Personality
The toughest native hardwood in Europe — slow-growing, iron-dense, and utterly implacable; Roman chariot-builders chose hornbeam for a reason, and it hasn't forgotten.

## Real-world facts
- **Typical / max height:** 15–25 m (Carpinus betulus); up to ~30 m
- **Lifespan:** 300+ years; coppiced specimens can persist far longer
- **Wood:** ~830 kg/m³ air-dry (range 530–830 kg/m³ depending on site); Janka values for close relatives (hop hornbeam) 4,810–6,903 N; widely cited as the hardest wood of any European native tree
- **Growth speed:** slow
- **Ecology / notable traits:** Hardest timber in Europe; historically used for chariot wheels, mill cogs, butchers' blocks, and tool handles; excellent coppice species — regenerates vigorously after cutting; tolerates shade and is a common woodland understory dominant; important for cavity-nesting birds; name "horn-beam" literally means "hard tree" (Old English)

## Combat flavor
Carpinus is the Juggernaut in every sense — slow to advance but nearly impossible to stop once moving, absorbing attacks with wood so dense it was used for mill gears. Signature ability: an ironclad passive that reduces incoming damage and reflects a portion back on melee strikers.

## Living creatures
Carpinus is the quiet workhorse of NW European ancient woodland — its retained dead leaves shelter overwintering invertebrates, and its hard seeds are the single most important winter food for the elusive hawfinch.

- **Hornbeam aphids (Myzocallis carpini / Aphididae)** — thousands per mature tree in summer; small yellow aphids producing the steady honeydew rain under hedged hornbeam. [Influential Points: Myzocallis carpini](https://influentialpoints.com/Gallery/Myzocallis_carpini_Hornbeam_aphid.htm)
- **Hazel leaf-miner moth (Stigmella microtheriella / Nepticulidae)** — hundreds of mines per mature tree; despite the name it works hazel and hornbeam in equal measure. [Wikipedia: Stigmella microtheriella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stigmella_microtheriella)
- **Nut-tree tussock (Colocasia coryli / Noctuidae)** — a handful of larvae per tree; one of the signature hornbeam/hazel moths and a striking ginger-tufted caterpillar. [UKmoths: Colocasia coryli](https://www.ukmoths.org.uk/species/colocasia-coryli/)
- **Generalist micro-moths (Phyllonorycter esperella & similar / Gracillariidae)** — a few hundred mines per mature tree; collectively the largest insect biomass after aphids. [Butterfly Conservation: Nut-tree Tussock](https://butterfly-conservation.org/moths/nut-tree-tussock)
- **Hornbeam gall mites (Aceria / Eriophyidae)** — easily tens of thousands per tree; producers of the small pouch-galls along the leaf veins. [Woodland Trust: Hornbeam](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hornbeam/)
- **Overwintering invertebrates in marcescent leaves (mixed Araneae, Hemiptera, Coleoptera)** — dozens to low hundreds per tree; retained brown leaves act as a dry winter refuge, a known reason hornbeam hedges are ecologically valuable. [Woodland Trust: Hornbeam](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hornbeam/)
- **Hawfinch (Coccothraustes coccothraustes)** — 1–2 birds (occasionally winter flocks of 10+) per fruiting tree; hornbeam seed is their preferred winter food and Carpinus woods are core hawfinch habitat in NW Europe. [Woodland Trust: Hornbeam](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hornbeam/)
- **Tits & finches at the samaras (Cyanistes, Parus, Fringilla)** — flocks of 5–20 working the seed clusters in autumn and winter. [Wildlife Trusts: Common hornbeam](https://www.wildlifetrusts.org/wildlife-explorer/trees-and-shrubs/common-hornbeam)
- **Eurasian Grey Squirrel (Sciurus carolinensis)** — 1–3 individuals stripping bark and eating buds and nutlets; squirrels are a real conservation pressure on Carpinus stems. [Woodland Trust: Hornbeam](https://www.woodlandtrust.org.uk/trees-woods-and-wildlife/british-trees/a-z-of-british-trees/hornbeam/)
- **Cavity-nesting birds & roosting bats (Picidae, Pipistrellus / Vespertilionidae)** — 1 nesting pair or roosting bat per old coppice stool; Carpinus's longevity and easy hollowing make it a top cavity provider in old parkland. [Bat Conservation Trust: Bats and woodland](https://www.bats.org.uk/our-work/landscapes-for-bats/bats-and-woodland)
